Context

Ongoing challenge in deliveries

Large orders remain a long-standing pain point for drivers, often leading to cancellations when they discover at the merchant that the order is too big to carry. Improving large order prediction is key, but until this capability matures, fulfilment rates will remain low.

Existing large order tagging vs reality

Large orders are identified based on either weight or basket size, with definitions varying by country. To manage complexity during batching, thresholds are set to prevent grouped orders from being classified as large.

Two sample receipts illustrate the gap: the more expensive order is not always the physically larger one. The system makes this call millions of times a day across a large catalogue — yet we often do not know exact weight, dimensions, or how the restaurant will package the order.

Allocation logic vs reality

Fulfilment strategy prioritises 4-wheeled vehicles for large orders (up to three attempts), then falls back to 2-wheelers and walkers. In Southeast Asia, 4W supply for Food and Mart is extremely limited: only 7% of large orders go to cars; 93% go to 2W riders. Large-order fulfilment was about 5 percentage points lower than non-large (July 2021), with about 2pp higher cancellations.

Large orders were 0.8% of Food orders with 89.24% fulfilment (~5% below non-large). In Mart, large orders were 3.13% of orders with 78.88% fulfilment (~11.12% below non-large). Unallocation and pre-allocation cancellations mattered; driver cancellations were ~1% higher for large orders in both categories.

Feedback

“Big orders are often undesirable because they may require additional trips.”

“We sometimes have to make multiple trips or cancel the job entirely, which is not ideal.”

“I often can’t carry large orders due to their size and weight. This either forces me to cancel the job, wasting time and effort or risk my safety trying to deliver items that aren’t suited for my vehicle. Currently, Grab doesn’t offer an alternative way for us to handle these orders effectively.”

Large orders pose major challenges for two-wheeled drivers: fewer viable jobs, lower AOV/GMV, and safety risks such as balance issues or damaged goods — hurting driver experience and customer satisfaction.

Opportunity

Despite the challenges, large orders carry high value: more items, higher AOV, and roughly triple the revenue of regular orders — driving GMV and margins. In Jan 2023, regional cancellation rates for large orders were significantly higher than “wait too long” cancellations.

Potential fulfilment rate gain

  • Mart: +11.62pp
  • Food: +5.6pp
  • Overall: ~2–3pp improvement

Potential GMV uplift from lower cancellations

  • Mart: ~$480K/mo
  • Food: ~$1.689M/mo

Improve driver sentiment

  • Minimise safety risks
  • Ease delivery load for driver partners
  • Reduce idle time from cancellations

Hypothesis

HMW improve the large order experience for 2W riders through clearer guidance and support?

Ideations

Driver-initiated split (MVP)

MVP approach to overcome challenges

  • Relies on drivers’ own judgment of capacity rather than the system alone.
  • Drivers can request a second driver if they cannot carry the entire order.
  • Minimises CTS by splitting only when necessary.

Incremental CTS from second drivers ~S$85K/mo.

CTS = Cost to Serve (driver pay).

Grab-initiated split

Orders automatically divided and assigned to two drivers. Challenges: low accuracy in large-order detection and capacity estimation.

  • Tagging improvements take time.
  • False positives drive high CTS — incremental CTS from second drivers ~S$920K/mo.

MVP approach

The MVP approach enables drivers to request assistance from another driver when they can’t complete a large order alone.

Competitive benchmark & comparative study

Foodpanda

On the driver side, a comparative study of Foodpanda’s large order experience revealed that:

  1. Drivers can request assistance by initiating a split (photo + help icon).
  2. Limited clarity on which items each driver should pick up — drivers default to what fits in bags.
  3. Pickup instructions clarify primary vs secondary roles; multiple riders allowed.
  4. No dedicated large-order flow — order detail depth appears after accept.

Room to improve coordination and clarity for large, multi-part deliveries.

Driver-oriented split

  1. Primary driver taps headphone icon to request another rider.
  2. Driver sends a photo when requesting the split.
  3. Not told item-level split — pick up what fits.
  4. Primary vs secondary clear from pickup instructions.
  5. Can be two riders or more.

DoorDash & Deliveroo

Western delivery apps often surface large, high-pay orders from the offer screen, for example a “High pay order!” banner, item count and distance, and a payout band, which reinforces value through pickup and completion. For the second rider, pickup copy can state clearly that another courier is helping on a large order so roles stay obvious.

Behaviour findings — research

We tested early concepts of the Large Order feature. While it shows strong potential, key challenges must be addressed for successful adoption:

Key objectives

  • Discover any behaviour that DAX (driver-partners) had toward this Large Order feature.
  • Validate the usability of the Large Order design.
  • Validate any concept within the Large Order design.

Research details

  • Method: 1:1 interviews with five 2W DAX in the UX Lab.
  • Country of research: Singapore.
  • Time period: 31 March 2023.

Our UT session in the UX Lab with 2W DAX.

Behaviour findings — discoverability & trust

Discoverability

  • Drivers often review merchant name, items, value, and bulky flags before accepting — but many overlook Request Extra Driver under More actions and on collection.
  • Direction: Surface that entry earlier so partners notice it before they cancel.

Trust

  • The same flows raised fear of unfair splits and sabotage when “half” of an order isn’t defined clearly.
  • Stories from other platforms amplified that worry (quick earnings vs teamwork).
  • Design challenge: Prevent abuse while keeping splits understandable — tie clarity to hand-off, bags, and roles.

Research & design feedback

Usability testing tied these themes into one thread: from whether drivers understood earnings on the way in, to how clear each in-flow screen was once they committed. On Path A (after arrive, blue card) and Path B (More actions), reassurance that pay wouldn’t drop rarely registered early enough — so the feature felt riskier than it was.

On “Need help with a large order?”, a tall stepper read as “wait for the system” while the real next step was tapping Request for Driver. We reframed hierarchy so the primary action owned the page. In the searching / waiting state, Design A (bullets under the active timeline step) beat Design B (small grey copy under the title) — drivers missed animated subcopy and needed scannable bullets.

After match, showing the second driver’s name and photo helped prove a split to customers but opened privacy and misuse concerns; we weighed reducing or hiding identity. Photo and receipt steps needed clearer purpose (who the proof is for) and a path for one receipt across two riders, plus honesty about merchant confusion when bags aren’t marked. On route, “Main driver” implied full liability; long waits at the merchant eroded patience — we pushed neutral role labels and realistic wait expectations.

Insight: Drivers don’t want to wait at the merchant for the other rider, and “main driver” reads as owning liability for the entire order.

Findings

  • Most drivers dislike waiting for the second driver at the merchant.
  • Some will wait about 15–20 minutes before it feels like wasted time.
  • The “main driver” label feels like being in charge; if the second driver fails, they worry they’ll be held responsible.

Recommendation: Rephrase “main driver” with more neutral role wording.

Design feedback — when allocation fails

Insight: Drivers would give up instead of reallocate if the first allocation fails — more time feels wasted.

Findings

  • Drivers don’t want to wait without clarity on what to do next. They cancel, or some deliver by themselves in two or more trips.
  • If they self-deliver across multiple trips, they expect compensation.
    • Today’s flow: complete trips first (while updating the customer), then claim through CS.

HMW give a clear action when the extra driver can’t be found? (e.g. a direct path to cancel, or options and copy for multi-trip self-delivery.)

“I will close, because no one wants to pick so why waste time.”

— DAX 2

Key insights

Skipped features

Some drivers pre-check orders via merchant name and order details before accepting — and can bypass Request Extra Driver under More actions and on order collection.

Trust and sabotage

Riders worry about splits when “half” isn’t defined by the system; some fear the first driver takes one bag and leaves the rest. Stories from other platforms reinforce that some riders prioritise quick earnings over teamwork.

Second-driver coordination

  • Wait time: many won’t wait more than ~15 minutes for a second driver.
  • Fallbacks: unclear next steps if no second driver (cancel, multi-trip, compensation).
  • Privacy: showing profiles can increase tension in conflicts.

Large order discovery & key principles

Determine three entry points and flows from driver behaviour

Streamline, hassle-free request flow

No proof or validation required to confirm a large order.

  1. Once submitted, the system handles follow-through — no extra driver follow-ups required.
  2. Avoid wasted time waiting for assignment mechanics.

Light-touch guidance

Encourage the first driver to take about half and help secure a receipt for the second driver — framed as benefits, not hard requirements.

Use tooltips to familiarise drivers with the icon and entry points.

Entry point 1 — before arriving

During peak hours or from merchant name, drivers often pre-check order size on Order details — some cancel early. Tooltip: “Get an extra driver for large orders after you arrive.” (Disabled before arrival; active after.)

Entry point 2 — cancellation

Drivers may cancel before or after arriving. Mitigation: when the order may be too large, surface Request for Extra Driver in the cancel path.

Modal: “Why not get an extra driver?” — “You’ll get the same earnings, and only need to deliver half of the order.” Primary: Request for Extra Driver · Secondary: Cancel anyway.

Entry point 3 — after arriving

After Arrived, on Check order, drivers may see volume they cannot carry; some back out and cancel. Tooltip on the extra-driver icon: “Get an extra driver for large orders.” Primary: I’ve checked.

Second driver experience

Second driver benefit: minimal wait — order ready for pickup on arrival. Always-on copy: you are the second driver; the first driver already collected part of the order; you collect what’s left. Sheet: “You’re the second driver for this split delivery” · Help Centre · Got it.

To reduce sabotage concerns, only the second driver could flag feedback if the order was still too large.

Impact

Highlights of Large Order

Regional fulfilment rate +1.3%
Cancellation rate −1.5%

The work took rounds of brainstorming and alignment with many stakeholders — entry points, visibility, and a clear story for drivers. The shipped experience reduced cancels drivers couldn’t sustain and eased daily overload; it touched customers, merchants, and drivers. Personally it deepened empathy for partners on the ground.

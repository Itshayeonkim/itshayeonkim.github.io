Context

Ongoing challenge in deliveries

Large orders remain a longstanding pain point for drivers, often leading to cancellations when they discover at the merchant that the order is too big to carry. Improving large order prediction is key, but until this capability matures, fulfilment rates will remain low.

Existing large order tagging vs reality

Large orders are identified based on either weight or basket size, with definitions varying by country. To manage complexity during batching, thresholds are set to prevent grouped orders from being classified as large.

Two sample receipts illustrate the gap: the more expensive order is not always the physically larger one. The system makes this call millions of times a day across a large catalogue — yet we often do not know exact weight, dimensions, or how the restaurant will package the order.

Allocation logic vs reality

Fulfilment strategy prioritises four wheeled vehicles for large orders (up to three attempts), then falls back to two wheelers and walkers. In Southeast Asia, 4W supply for Food and Mart is extremely limited: only 7% of large orders go to cars; 93% go to 2W riders. Large order fulfilment was about 5 percentage points lower than for smaller orders (July 2021), with about 2pp higher cancellations.

Large orders were 0.8% of Food orders with 89.24% fulfilment (~5% below smaller orders). In Mart, large orders were 3.13% of orders with 78.88% fulfilment (~11.12% below smaller orders). Unallocation and preallocation cancellations mattered; driver cancellations were ~1% higher for large orders in both categories.

Large orders pose major challenges for drivers on two wheelers: fewer viable jobs, lower AOV/GMV, and safety risks such as balance issues or damaged goods. This hurts driver experience and customer satisfaction.

Opportunity

Despite the challenges, large orders carry high value: more items, higher AOV, and roughly triple the revenue of regular orders, driving GMV and margins. In Jan 2023, regional cancellation rates for large orders were significantly higher than “wait too long” cancellations.

  • Overall: ~2–3pp improvement

Potential GMV uplift from lower cancellations

Improve driver sentiment

  • Minimise safety risks

Hypothesis

HMW improve the large order experience for 2W riders through clearer guidance and support?

Ideations

Driver initiated split (MVP)

MVP approach to overcome challenges

  • Relies on drivers’ own judgment of capacity rather than the system alone.
  • Drivers can request a second driver if they cannot carry the entire order.
  • Minimises CTS by splitting only when necessary.

Incremental CTS from second drivers ~S$85K/mo.

CTS = Cost to Serve (driver pay).

Grab initiated split

Orders automatically divided and assigned to two drivers. Challenges: low accuracy in large order detection and capacity estimation.

  • Tagging improvements take time.
  • False positives drive high CTS — incremental CTS from second drivers ~S$920K/mo.

MVP approach

The MVP approach enables drivers to request assistance from another driver when they can’t complete a large order alone.

Competitive benchmark & comparative study

Foodpanda

On 15 February our team placed a large McDonald’s order, and when the riders arrived I ran short interviews with them. Through those conversations we could see that Foodpanda already runs a split that drivers initiate, and we could follow how that process works in practice.

On the driver side, a comparative study of Foodpanda’s large order experience revealed that the contextual photo and notes to the right summarise desk findings and the split mechanics we saw in the driver experience.

What the study surfaced

  1. Requesting help: Drivers can start a split by tapping the help or headphone icon and sending a photo so Foodpanda can assign another rider.
  2. What to pick up: The app gives limited guidance on which items belong to which rider. People default to loading what fits in the bag.
  3. Pickup instructions: Copy still makes primary and secondary roles clear at pickup, and two riders or more can share one large order.
  4. When detail appears: There is no journey built only for large orders. Item counts and richer fields tend to show after accept, not on the first offer card.
  5. Implication: These findings leave room to improve coordination and clarity for drivers on large deliveries with many parts.

DoorDash & Deliveroo

Western delivery apps often surface large, high pay orders from the offer screen, for example a “High pay order!” banner, item count and distance, and a payout band, which reinforces value through pickup and completion. For the second rider, pickup copy can state clearly that another courier is helping on a large order so roles stay obvious.

Behaviour findings — research

We tested early concepts of the Large Order feature. While it shows strong potential, key challenges must be addressed for successful adoption:

Key objectives

  • Discover any behaviour that DAX (driver partners) had toward this Large Order feature.
  • Validate the usability of the Large Order design.
  • Validate any concept within the Large Order design.

Research details

  • Method: 1:1 interviews with five 2W DAX in the UX Lab.
  • Country of research: Singapore.
  • Time period: 31 March 2023.

Our UT session in the UX Lab with 2W DAX.

Behaviour findings — discoverability & trust

Discoverability

  • Drivers often review merchant name, items, value, and bulky flags before accepting — but many overlook Request Extra Driver under More actions and on collection.
  • Direction: Surface that entry earlier so partners notice it before they cancel.

Trust

  • The same flows raised fear of unfair splits and sabotage when “half” of an order isn’t defined clearly.
  • Stories from other platforms amplified that worry (quick earnings vs teamwork).
  • Design challenge: Prevent abuse while keeping splits understandable — tie clarity to handoff, bags, and roles.

Research & design feedback

Usability testing connected one theme across the full journey. We checked whether drivers understood earnings before they committed and whether each screen in the flow stayed clear after they committed. In Path A (after arrive, blue card) and Path B (More actions), reassurance about stable pay often appeared too late, so the feature felt riskier than intended.

On “Need help with a large order?”, a tall stepper looked like a passive wait state, while the true next action was Request for Driver. We changed hierarchy so the main action led the page. In the searching and waiting state, Design A with bullets under the active timeline step worked better than Design B with small grey copy under the title. Drivers missed animated copy and needed scannable bullets.

After match, showing the second driver’s name and photo helped prove the split to customers, but it raised privacy and misuse concerns, so we reviewed where identity should be reduced or hidden. Photo and receipt steps also needed clearer purpose, including who needs the proof and how one receipt should work across two riders, plus clearer communication for merchant confusion when bags are not marked. On route, the “Main driver” label implied full liability, and long waits at the merchant reduced patience, so we shifted to neutral role labels and realistic wait expectations.

Findings

  • Most drivers dislike waiting for the second driver at the merchant.
  • Some will wait about 15–20 minutes before it feels like wasted time.
  • The “main driver” label feels like being in charge; if the second driver fails, they worry they’ll be held responsible.

Design feedback — when allocation fails

Insight: Drivers would give up instead of reallocate if the first allocation fails — more time feels wasted.

Findings

  • Drivers don’t want to wait without clarity on what to do next. They cancel, or some deliver by themselves in two or more trips.
  • If they deliver themselves across multiple trips, they expect compensation.

Key insights

Key findings

Skipped features

Some drivers precheck orders via merchant name and order details before accepting — and can bypass Request Extra Driver under More actions and on order collection.

Trust and sabotage

Riders worry about splits when “half” isn’t defined by the system; some fear the first driver takes one bag and leaves the rest. Stories from other platforms reinforce that some riders prioritise quick earnings over teamwork.

Second driver coordination

  • Wait time: many won’t wait more than ~15 minutes for a second driver.
  • Fallbacks: unclear next steps if no second driver (cancel, several trips, compensation).
  • Privacy: showing profiles can increase tension in conflicts.

Large order discovery and key principles

Three entry points shaped by real driver behavior

Simple request flow

Let drivers ask for help without proof gates or long checks.

  1. After they submit, the system carries assignment through to delivery so the first rider is not chasing status by hand.
  2. Keep wait states short so riders are not stuck guessing at the merchant.

Clear & Light touch guidance

Suggest a fair share of items for the first rider and a clear receipt path for the second rider. Keep the tone helpful, not punitive.

Use tooltips to explain the icon, what it does, and when each entry point is available.

Entry point 1 — before arriving

During peak hours, or after checking the merchant name, drivers often precheck order size on Order details. Some cancel early. Tooltip copy: “Get an extra driver for large orders after you arrive.” It is disabled before arrival and active after arrival.

Entry point 2 — cancellation

Drivers may cancel before or after arriving. Mitigation: when the order may be too large, surface Request for Extra Driver in the cancel path.

Entry point 3 — after arriving

Once drivers are at the merchant and tap Arrived, they land on Check order, where the app asks them to confirm the order is correct (customer, items, total). This is often the first moment they see that volume exceeds what they can carry, so some back out and cancel. Beside the order ID, the add driver icon shows a tooltip: “Get an extra driver for large orders.”

Second driver experience

Second driver benefit: minimal wait, with the order ready for pickup on arrival. Always on copy states that you are the second driver, the first driver already collected part of the order, and you collect what is left. Sheet copy: “You’re the second driver for this split delivery”, with Help Centre and Got it actions.

To reduce sabotage concerns, only the second driver could flag feedback if the order was still too large.

Impact

Highlights of Large Order

Regional fulfilment rate +1.3%
Cancellation rate −1.5%

The work took rounds of brainstorming and alignment with many stakeholders on entry points, visibility, and a clear story for drivers. The shipped experience reduced cancels that drivers could not sustain and eased daily overload across customers, merchants, and drivers. Personally, it deepened empathy for partners on the ground.

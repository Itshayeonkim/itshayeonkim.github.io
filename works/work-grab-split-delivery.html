Ongoing challenge in deliveries

Large orders remain a longstanding pain point for drivers, often leading to cancellations when they discover at the merchant that the order is too big to carry. Improving large order prediction is key, but until this capability matures, fulfilment rates will remain low.

Existing large order tagging vs reality

Large orders are identified based on either weight or basket size, with definitions varying by country. To manage complexity during batching, thresholds are set to prevent grouped orders from being classified as large.

Two sample receipts illustrate the gap: the more expensive order is not always the physically larger one. The system makes this call millions of times a day across a large catalogue — yet we often do not know exact weight, dimensions, or how the restaurant will package the order.

Allocation logic vs reality

Fulfilment strategy prioritises four wheeled vehicles for large orders (up to three attempts), then falls back to two wheelers and walkers. In Southeast Asia, 4W supply for Food and Mart is extremely limited: only 7% of large orders go to cars; 93% go to 2W riders. Large order fulfilment was about 5 percentage points lower than for smaller orders (July 2021), with about 2pp higher cancellations.

Large orders were 0.8% of Food orders with 89.24% fulfilment (~5% below smaller orders). In Mart, large orders were 3.13% of orders with 78.88% fulfilment (~11.12% below smaller orders). Unallocation and preallocation cancellations mattered; driver cancellations were ~1% higher for large orders in both categories.

Feedback

  • “Big orders are often undesirable because they may require additional trips.”
  • “We sometimes have to make multiple trips or cancel the job entirely, which is not ideal.”
  • “I often can’t carry large orders due to their size and weight. This either forces me to cancel the job, wasting time and effort or risk my safety trying to deliver items that aren’t suited for my vehicle. Currently, Grab doesn’t offer an alternative way for us to handle these orders effectively.”

Large orders pose major challenges for drivers on two wheelers: fewer viable jobs, lower AOV/GMV, and safety risks such as balance issues or damaged goods. This hurts driver experience and customer satisfaction.

Opportunity

Despite the challenges, large orders carry high value: more items, higher AOV, and roughly triple the revenue of regular orders, driving GMV and margins. In Jan 2023, regional cancellation rates for large orders were significantly higher than “wait too long” cancellations.

Ideations

Driver initiated split (MVP)

MVP approach to overcome challenges

  • Relies on drivers’ own judgment of capacity rather than the system alone.
  • Drivers can request a second driver if they cannot carry the entire order.
  • Minimises CTS by splitting only when necessary.

Incremental CTS from second drivers ~S$85K/mo.

CTS = Cost to Serve (driver pay).

Grab initiated split

Orders automatically divided and assigned to two drivers. Challenges: low accuracy in large order detection and capacity estimation.

  • Tagging improvements take time.
  • False positives drive high CTS — incremental CTS from second drivers ~S$920K/mo.

The MVP approach enables drivers to request assistance from another driver when they can’t complete a large order alone.

Behaviour findings

In UX Lab usability sessions with 2W DAX, we tested early Large Order concepts. Those sessions validated direction and surfaced themes we carried into discoverability and trust. The feature shows strong potential, but key challenges must still be addressed for adoption.

Research

Key objectives

  • Discover any behaviour that DAX (driver partners) had toward this Large Order feature.
  • Validate the usability of the Large Order design.
  • Validate any concept within the Large Order design.

Research details

  • Method: 1:1 interviews with five 2W DAX in the UX Lab.
  • Country of research: Singapore.
  • Time period: 31 March 2023.

Discoverability & trust

Discoverability

  • Behaviour findings: When drivers receive an order, they usually check the merchant name first, then open order details to see how many items are in the batch, drawing on patterns they have picked up from experience. In most cases, however, they only fully grasp what they are picking up once they arrive at the merchant.
  • Decision: We surfaced the new split delivery entry on the order detail page drivers already review and on the page after they pressed ‘Arrived’ button after they reached at the merchant (where they naturally look), so the feature is easier to discover.

Trust

  • On what worried them most (unfair splits and sabotage), drivers asked for clearer rules on how a large order would be divided between partners. Some said they did not want to repeat experiences they had already had on Foodpanda.
  • Some drivers also worried whether their earnings would be split; we planned to explain that in depth through new feature education.
  • Frustration lingered over why all liability seemed to sit with drivers. There was also an urgent need for a fallback when allocation fails, a gap in the current flow.

Research & design feedback

Entry point bypassing issue

Drivers had two ways to request an extra driver. Path A: tap Arrive, go through Collect, and read a blue card. Path B: go back to the index page and open More actions. Neither path communicated earnings clearly. On Path A, drivers didn’t read the blue card carefully and only realised how earnings held up at the end. On Path B, the blue card never appeared, so they missed the message entirely. Some also prechecked orders via merchant name and order details, which let them quietly bypass Request Extra Driver altogether under More actions and on collection.

Stepper hid the real next action

On “Need help with a large order?”, drivers were slow to proceed because the page had two competing parts: a stepper (A) in the main content area, and a bottom Request for Driver button (B). Many focused on the stepper first and assumed they should wait for something to happen instead of taking any action on the page. We changed the hierarchy so it was unmistakable: the main action is to tap the button, not wait on the stepper.

Drivers scanned, didn’t read

In the searching and waiting state, Design A with bullets under the active timeline step worked better than Design B with small grey copy under the title. Drivers missed animated copy and needed scannable bullets.

Trust came from rules, not faces

Showing the second driver’s name and photo helped prove the split to customers, but raised privacy and misuse concerns. Trust ran deeper than identity: when “half” wasn’t defined by the system, riders worried about unfair splits and feared the first driver would take one bag and leave the rest. We reviewed where identity should be reduced and clarified the split rules.

Receipt, photo, and merchant handoff

Photo and receipt steps needed clearer purpose: who needs the proof, how one receipt works across two riders, and how to clear up merchant confusion when bags aren’t marked.

Driver’s sentiment toward this new feature

Three concerns came up across usability sessions: waiting at the merchant, the “main driver” label, and what happens when allocation fails.

How to improve

HMW improve the large order split experience for 2W riders with clear guidance and being more supportive?

Two principles, drawn from the driver concerns above, guided the design that followed.

  1. 01

    Simple request, no extra burden

    Drivers ask for help without proof gates or long checks, and finding the second driver is not their job. They pushed back on this strongly in usability testing.

    • Once they tap Request, their part is done; the system handles allocation through to delivery.
  2. 02

    Clear & light-touch guidance

    Suggest a fair share for the first rider and a clear receipt path for the second. Tone stays helpful, not punitive.

    • Tooltips explain the icon, what it does, and when each entry point is available.

3 Entry points

1

Entry Point 1 — Before arriving

During peak hours or after checking the merchant name, drivers often precheck order size on Order details and may cancel early. The tooltip explains that they can get an extra driver after arrival, so the action stays disabled before arrival and becomes active after they arrive.

2

Entry Point 2 — Cancellation

Drivers may cancel before or after arriving. When the order looks too large, the cancel path surfaces Request for Extra Driver as a clear alternative.

3

Entry Point 3 — After arriving

After tapping Arrived, drivers land on Check order to confirm customer, items, and total. This is often the first moment they realize the volume exceeds what they can carry, so some back out and cancel. Beside the order ID, the add driver icon offers a tooltip: “Get an extra driver for large orders.”

Second driver experience

To make the role clear, the second driver is labeled on Order details from the start, and a sheet on arrival reconfirms it: “You’re the second driver for this split delivery”.

To reduce sabotage concerns, only the second driver could flag feedback if the order was still too large.

Takeaways

The work took rounds of brainstorming and alignment with many stakeholders on entry points, visibility, and a clear story for drivers. The shipped experience reduced cancels that drivers could not sustain and eased daily overload across customers, merchants, and drivers. Personally, it deepened empathy for partners on the ground.
